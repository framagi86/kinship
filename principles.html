Core Principles

The Seven Laws & Three Freedoms

Two sets of principles guide Kinship: the Seven Laws of Ecology and the Three Fundamental Freedoms. Together, they provide the lens through which we approach living as kin to all life.

The Seven Laws of Ecology

John Michael Greer articulated seven laws describing how living systems function. These aren't prescriptions for how to live—they're observable patterns in how life works. Cultures that ignore or actively work against these laws ultimately fail. Cultures that respect and align with them have the possibility of thriving.

1

The Law of Wholes

Everything that exists is part of a whole system and depends on the health of the whole system for its own existence. It thrives only if the whole system thrives, and it cannot harm the whole system without harming itself.

While our culture reduces all things to their parts, the universe functions in wholes. This is the basic difference between being reductionist and being holistic. The fundamental implication: it's not possible to truly know something without considering how it fits into its broader context.

Consequently, we—alongside everything else—cannot truly thrive unless the whole context in which we live thrives. If we pollute the air to industrially produce consumer goods, or use technology reliant on polluting energy, we're ultimately shooting ourselves in the foot. When we imagine ourselves separate from the rest of the world, entitled to take what we want without consequence, we violate this fundamental law.

For Kinship, this means recognizing ourselves as inseparable from the web of life. Our wellbeing depends on the wellbeing of ecosystems, other species, water, soil, air. We cannot extract and accumulate without harming the whole—and therefore harming ourselves.

2

The Law of Flow

Everything that exists is created and sustained by flows of matter, energy, and information that come from the whole system to which it belongs and that return to that whole system. Participating in these flows, without interfering with them, brings health and wholeness; blocking them, in an attempt to turn flows into accumulations, causes suffering and disruption to the whole system and all its parts.

This second law builds on Erich Fromm's distinction between having and being. Accumulation is fundamentally about having, while participating in flows is fundamentally about being. The problem with emphasizing having over being is that possession is itself a fiction. We can be, and can do so without effort; but can we find anything we have ever truly possessed?

In nature, if something accumulates, a correction takes place to restore flow. This is visible in countless ways: water pools until it overflows or evaporates; nutrients concentrate until decomposers redistribute them; populations expand until resources limit them. The correction is sometimes gentle, sometimes catastrophic, but it always occurs.

Our culture is built on accumulation: wealth, possessions, land, power, knowledge treated as property. We dam rivers, hoard resources, create artificial scarcities. This generates immense suffering—for those without access to what's been accumulated, for the systems disrupted by extraction, and ultimately for those who accumulate, who find that possession brings anxiety rather than satisfaction.

Kinship aims to give us space to be rather than to accumulate. This means participating in flows—receiving what we need, using it well, returning it to the system. It means rejecting the logic of accumulation in all its forms.

3

The Law of Balance

Everything that exists can continue to exist only by being in balance with itself, with other things, and with the whole system of which it is part. That balance is not found by going to one extreme or the other or by remaining fixed at a static point; it is created by self-correcting movements to either side of a midpoint.

In nature, if something accumulates, a correction takes place to restore flow. Balance isn't about reaching a fixed point and staying there—it's about constant adjustment. Think of standing on one foot: you don't achieve balance and then freeze. You constantly make small corrections, swaying slightly this way and that, always moving through the midpoint rather than stopping at it.

This law suggests that balance is useful as a verb, not a noun. We practice the art of balancing: notice you're in an extreme, apply a corrective action, pass through the midpoint recognizing you'll swing toward the other extreme, repeat. The goal isn't to eliminate movement but to become skillful at the dance.

In social systems, this means avoiding rigid structures that prevent natural corrections. When we try to fix things in place—whether social hierarchies, economic arrangements, or settlement patterns—we prevent the self-correcting movements that maintain health. Instead of forcing stability, we allow dynamic equilibrium.

4

The Law of Limits

Everything that exists has limits, and these limits define what it is and what it can do. These limits are not arbitrary restrictions but the very source of form, power, and meaning.

Limits aren't obstacles to be overcome but sources of possibility. A bird flies precisely because of its physical limitations, not despite them. If birds had no limits, they would be formless and capable of nothing. The specific limits of hollow bones, feathered wings, and lightweight bodies make flight possible.

Our culture treats limits as problems to solve through technology and growth. We imagine we can transcend ecological limits through ingenuity, expand endlessly, consume without consequence. This is fantasy. Limits are real, and attempting to ignore them doesn't make them disappear—it simply ensures we encounter them catastrophically rather than gracefully.

When we accept limits in each activity—cultivation, foraging, hunting, crafts—we prevent specialization and maintain human flexibility. If we cultivate only as much as we can without depleting soil, forage only what regenerates naturally, craft only what we actually need, we preserve diverse skills and prevent dependence on any single activity. This is what I mean by an ecology of freedom: accepting limits in each part creates freedom in the whole.

The Law of Limits enables freedom by forcing diversity rather than constraining it. This reverses how we typically think about limits and freedom—but it's how life actually works.

5

The Law of Cause & Effect

Everything that exists is a result of causes, and everything that happens becomes the cause of other effects. Effects always follow from causes, and equal causes produce similar effects.

The adage might be restated as "What you sow, you reap." It's important to align methods and goals, and to gain understanding of cause-effect relationships in the systems we inhabit.

The second principle is similarity in scale: "Great oaks from little acorns grow." Large, complex effects require sufficient accumulation of causes of similar scale. This law is both a diagnostic tool and a mandate for patience.

In practical terms, if we want resilient, ecologically integrated communities, we can't create them through methods that violate ecological principles. If we want freedom, we can't build it through structures of control. If we want simplicity, we can't achieve it through complex systems. The means shape the ends—not perfectly, not always, but fundamentally.

This law also reminds us that small actions compound over time. We don't need to create massive change immediately. Consistent practice, aligned with principles, accumulates causes that eventually produce the effects we seek.

6

The Law of Planes

Everything in existence exists and functions on one of several planes of being or is composed of things from more than one plane acting together as a whole system. These planes are discrete, not continuous, and the passage of influences from one plane to another can take place only under conditions defined by the relationship of the planes involved.

Giving primacy to the physical world is a historical anomaly. Since time immemorial, human beings have understood that reality is composed of multiple planes. Beyond the physical, there are also: the etheric plane (the plane on which living things exist and from which life-force or vitality emerges); the astral plane (the plane of culture, desire, and emotion); and the mental plane (the plane of mind, thought, and abstract patterns).

The key insight: problems manifesting on one plane cannot always be solved by interventions on another plane alone. The planes are interwoven, but distinct. A problem that manifests physically may have roots in cultural patterns (astral) or ways of thinking (mental). Addressing only the physical symptoms without addressing other planes often fails.

This doesn't mean we need to become experts in working across planes. It means recognizing that reality is more complex than materialism admits, that cultural and spiritual dimensions matter, and that solutions must sometimes address multiple planes simultaneously. For Kinship, it means being open to practices that work on different planes—not just physical techniques but cultural practices, ways of thinking, spiritual approaches.

7

The Law of Evolution

Everything that exists comes into being by a process of evolution. That process starts with adaptation to changing conditions and ends with the establishment of a steady state of balance with its surroundings, following a threefold rhythm of challenge, response, and reintegration. Evolution is gradual rather than sudden, and it works by increasing diversity and accumulating possibilities.

Everything that exists comes into being through adaptation to changing conditions. Challenge prompts response; successful responses get reintegrated into the system, creating new possibilities for future adaptation. This is how species evolve, how ecosystems develop, how cultures change.

At its core, this law means that species and systems adapt to their environment through a constant process of challenge, response, and reintegration. We are not the apex of evolution, nor has evolution stopped for us. We continue to adapt—or fail to adapt—to changing conditions.

If we are to thrive long-term, we must expose ourselves to the world we live in. Without difficulties, the cycle of challenge, response, and reintegration cannot take place, and we become fragile rather than resilient. This doesn't mean seeking unnecessary hardship, but it does mean accepting that some difficulty is essential for adaptation.

Kinship needs to be a place that allows evolution to take place, while steering clear of excess. We expose ourselves to ecological realities—weather, seasons, physical work, material limits—not as punishment but as the conditions under which we adapt and become more capable. We avoid both the fragility of complete comfort and the destructiveness of unnecessary extremes.

The Three Fundamental Freedoms

David Graeber and David Wengrow's anthropological work reveals that throughout most of human history, people exercised three freedoms that modern civilization has largely abandoned. These aren't utopian fantasies or modern inventions—they're basic capacities humans practiced for tens of thousands of years. Kinship exists to reclaim and practice these freedoms.

1. The Freedom to Move

The freedom to leave oppressive situations, to relocate, to change one's circumstances. Not being bound to a place, job, or social arrangement against one's will.

For most of human history, if a situation became intolerable—whether an abusive leader, resource scarcity, or social conflict—people could simply leave. They moved to new territories, joined different groups, created new settlements. This mobility was a fundamental check on power: leaders who became tyrannical found themselves leading no one.

Modern life severely constrains this freedom. We're bound by mortgages, employment contracts, visa requirements, property ownership, family obligations structured around permanent settlement. Moving requires immense resources and disrupts everything we've built. The freedom to move has become a privilege of the wealthy rather than a basic human capacity.

Kinship restores this freedom by avoiding fixed structures that bind people in place. Whether we remain mobile or settle, the principle is that no one is trapped. People stay because they choose to, not because leaving would mean losing everything.

2. The Freedom to Disobey

The freedom to refuse commands, to question authority, to maintain individual sovereignty. Not being coerced into compliance with rules or hierarchies one doesn't consent to.

In many historical societies, leadership was situational and consensual. People followed those with relevant expertise for specific tasks but maintained the right to refuse, question, or simply ignore authority they found illegitimate. Chiefs might give commands, but people could decline to follow them without punishment.

Modern systems demand obedience. Employment requires following orders. Governments enforce compliance through police and courts. Social pressure punishes those who question norms. We're taught from childhood that disobedience is wrong, that questioning authority is antisocial, that we must accept the rules of the systems we inhabit.

Kinship practices dissensus rather than consensus—individual sovereignty within shared ecological commitments. We don't demand agreement or obedience. We maintain the right to disagree, to refuse participation in decisions we oppose, to question any authority that emerges. The only non-negotiable commitments are to the Seven Laws and to not harming the collective's ability to function.

3. The Freedom to Create New Social Worlds

The freedom to imagine and implement entirely different ways of organizing social life. Not being locked into inherited structures but able to experiment with new arrangements.

Throughout history, human societies have been wildly experimental. People constantly invented new ways of organizing themselves—different governance structures, economic arrangements, kinship systems, seasonal variations in social organization. This experimentation was normal, not exceptional.

We're told that current arrangements—states, capitalism, nuclear families, permanent settlements, formal law—are natural or inevitable. That all alternatives have been tried and failed. That we must accept the systems we inherit and work within them. This is false. Human social arrangements have always been more diverse and experimental than we're taught to believe.

Kinship exists to exercise this freedom. We're creating something different—not by blueprinting it in advance but by experimenting with alternatives to mainstream arrangements. We follow the Seven Laws as our foundation but remain open to discovering new forms of social organization through practice. We're not trying to return to any historical model but to explore what becomes possible when we reject the claim that current arrangements are inevitable.

A Shift in Perception

These principles aren't rules to follow but a lens for seeing differently. When we recognize ourselves as part of whole systems, participating in flows rather than accumulating, accepting limits as sources of freedom, understanding cause and effect, acknowledging multiple planes of reality, and exposing ourselves to challenge—everything changes. The three freedoms become not just theoretical possibilities but practical capacities we can actually exercise.
