Texts & Influences

These texts and thinkers shape Kinship's philosophical foundation. For fuller discussion of key influences, see the complete proposal.

Philosophical Influences

Stoic philosophy (especially Epictetus) on the dichotomy of control and living according to nature. Taoist philosophy (Dao De Jing and Zhuangzi) on wu wei and working with rather than against natural processes. Ecological thinking through John Michael Greer's Seven Laws. Anarchist anthropology through David Graeber and David Wengrow's work on the three fundamental freedoms.

Key Texts

Marshall Sahlins

The Original Affluent Society

Sahlins's groundbreaking essay challenges the assumption that hunter-gatherer societies lived in poverty and scarcity. By examining anthropological data, he demonstrates that many foraging societies actually enjoyed abundance—not through producing more, but through wanting less. They worked fewer hours, had more leisure time, and experienced less anxiety about material security than people in agricultural or industrial societies. This work fundamentally questions our assumptions about progress, wealth, and what constitutes a good life.

Ronald Wright

A Short History of Progress

Wright examines how civilizations fall into "progress traps"—situations where short-term solutions create long-term crises. Success at one stage creates vulnerabilities at the next. Agricultural abundance leads to population growth that demands more agriculture; technological innovation creates dependencies that become inescapable; complexity requires ever more energy to maintain. Wright shows this pattern recurring across civilizations from ancient Sumer to Easter Island to modern industrial society, helping us understand why solutions often become problems and how to recognize the pattern before it's too late.

Spencer Wells

Pandora's Seed: The Unforeseen Cost of Civilization

Wells explores how agriculture—our species' most transformative innovation—created as many problems as it solved. The shift from foraging to farming brought chronic disease, social hierarchy, warfare, and environmental degradation alongside its benefits of food security and population growth. Using genetic and anthropological evidence, Wells shows that many modern health problems stem from the mismatch between our evolved biology and agricultural/industrial lifestyles. His work helps us understand why returning to some "primitive" practices might not be regression but adaptation to our actual needs.

Epictetus

Discourses and Enchiridion

Teaching on the dichotomy of control and living according to nature—distinguishing between what lies within our power and what doesn't.

Erich Fromm

To Have or To Be?

Distinguishes between two fundamental modes of existence: having (accumulating, possessing) and being (experiencing, relating). Essential for understanding why Kinship emphasizes participation in flows over accumulation.

Masanobu Fukuoka

The One-Straw Revolution

Natural farming method beginning with "What is it NOT necessary to do?" Demonstrates that doing less can yield more, that limits create possibility, that our role is to partner with nature.

David Graeber

Debt: The First 5,000 Years and Bullshit Jobs

Demonstrates that our current economic system is neither natural nor inevitable. Explores meaningless work and its psychological toll.

David Graeber and David Wengrow

The Dawn of Everything

Demonstrates that for most of human history, people exercised three freedoms: to move, to disobey, and to create new social arrangements. Shows that social arrangements have always been more diverse and experimental than we're taught.

John Michael Greer

The Ecotechnic Future, The Long Descent, A World Full of Gods

How societies might adapt to declining resources, understanding catabolic collapse, and opening space for diverse spiritual practices and ways of knowing.

Ivan Illich

Tools for Conviviality

Distinguishes between convivial tools (which enhance autonomy) and manipulative tools (which create dependency). Explains why Kinship emphasizes simple, convivial approaches.

Theodore Kaczynski

Technological Slavery

Rigorous critique of how technological systems constrain freedom and create dependency. Controversial but deserving serious engagement.

William Ophuls

Immoderate Greatness

Why complex civilizations inevitably overextend and collapse. Success breeds conditions for failure: growth demands more resources, complexity requires more energy.

Daniel Quinn

Ishmael

Examines the stories cultures tell themselves. Distinguishes between "Leavers" (living within ecological context) and "Takers" (taking regardless of consequences).

Stoics: Seneca, Marcus Aurelius, Musonius Rufus

Letters on simplicity, meditations on accepting what comes while acting with virtue, teachings on self-sufficiency. Demonstrate that limitation need not mean impoverishment.

Laozi and Zhuangzi

Dao De Jing and Zhuangzi

Wu wei (effortless action), working with reality's grain, limits of language and fixed categories. The way that can be named is not the eternal way.
